| **TECHNICAL BACKGROUND: Background of the Study**    Various studies have emphasized the importance of safe following distance in ensuring road safety and minimizing the risk of collisions. In the context of the Philippines (PH) or similar densely populated urban environments, managing safe following distances is particularly critical due to heavy traffic, frequent stop-and-go situations, and the prevalence of aggressive driving behavior. Previous research has identified that many road accidents in these regions are attributed to inadequate following distances. For instance, a study by Cruz and Santos ​\[1\]​ conducted in Metro Manila investigated the analysis of the safe following distance of vehicles in dense urban traffic. It highlighted the challenges posed by the local driving conditions. Understanding the factors influencing safe following distances and evaluating the current practices in the PH context is crucial for designing effective driver assistance systems to enhance road safety and mitigate the impact of traffic-related incidents.         Driver Assistance Systems (DAS) have emerged as a promising technology in contemporary automotive engineering, offering substantial support to drivers in various traffic scenarios. These systems employ a combination of sensors, cameras, and sophisticated algorithms to monitor the surroundings and detect potential hazards in real time. Tanaka et al. ​\[2\]​ demonstrated advancements in webcam-based DAS, providing accessible and cost-effective solutions to implement such safety features, especially in vehicles without built-in advanced driver assistance capabilities. By integrating DAS into cars, drivers can benefit from features like collision warning, adaptive cruise control, and lane departure warning, which can contribute to improved road safety and reduced accident rates. Investigating the integration of DAS in the Philippines and similar settings can help identify specific challenges and opportunities for effectively enhancing road safety through driver assistance technologies.                 The recent developments in Machine Learning (ML) and Artificial Intelligence (AI) have significantly transformed the field of computer vision and object detection, enabling real-time and accurate analysis of webcam feeds. Liu et al. ​\[3\]​ introduced YOLOv4, an advanced object detection algorithm that optimizes the speed and accuracy of identifying objects, including vehicles, within a given scene. Leveraging ML and AI capabilities, researchers and developers have successfully employed YOLO-based models for vehicle tracking, contributing to the progress of driver assistance systems. These technologies enable continuous monitoring of vehicle positions and distances in dynamic traffic scenarios, enhancing the safety and responsiveness of the assistance system. By exploring the integration of ML, AI, and YOLO in the proposed webcam-based tracking system, the study aims to offer valuable insights into the feasibility and effectiveness of such techniques for driver assistance and road safety in the Philippines. |  |
| **Statement of the Problem STATEMENT OF THE PROBLEM:**  The study aims to implement real-time vehicle tracking and distance calculation on webcams for driver assistance systems. It seeks to address the following specific problems:  Creation of a dataset of unique Philippine vehicles, such as jeepneys, to be identified by the algorithm.  Development of an application that can:  Track and identify all the vehicles in the frame.  Calculate the distance of the camera from the vehicle in front.  Alert drivers if the required safe following distance is not followed. To evaluate the developed application based on ISO 2500 standards using the Functionality, Usability, Reliability, Performance, and Security (FURPS) model  |  |
| **HOW DID OTHERS SOLVE THE PROBLEM? **           Elsagheer Mohamed, Alshalfan, Al-Hagery, and Othman ​\[4\]​ proposed an Internet of Vehicles (IoV) based technique to enable connected vehicles with cameras to determine the Safe Driving Distance and Speed to avoid forward collisions. It considers vehicles with and without Autonomous Emergency Braking (AEB) systems and takes various factors into account. Experimental results show that the method effectively calculates the Safe Driving Distance and maximizes road utilization by dynamically adjusting the minimum required safe gap based on current parameters like surrounding vehicle speed, road conditions, and weather. They focused on finding the Ideal Safe Driving Distance and Optimal Safe Driving Speed based on the Internet of Vehicles (IoV) to prevent forward collisions caused by tailgating. They explored various measurement techniques for real-time Assured Clear Distance Ahead (ACDA) in the IoV and autonomous vehicle environment. It comprehensively examines factors affecting Stopping Distance, including braking dynamics, and considers parameters influencing safe following distance and speed, such as vehicle speeds, separation distance, deceleration, driver reaction time, road and weather conditions, vehicle mass, braking force, and tire state. They also analyzed the impact of Autonomous Emergency Braking (AEB) systems and investigated the effect of sudden stops of followed vehicles on safe driving distance and speed. Additionally, they formulated using IoV emergency safety messages to exchange parameters between vehicles, enabling each vehicle to calculate the ideal safe following distance and speed based on current conditions. The findings aim to serve as a basis for developing applications related to safe driving and detecting safe driving violations. They emphasized the importance of vehicle-to-vehicle communication in connected and autonomous vehicles to exchange real-time data for collision avoidance.                 Olaverri-Monreal, Krizek, Michaeler, Lorenz, and Pichler ​\[5\]​ presented an innovative system that identifies vehicles and provides a rear-end distance warning to avoid collisions. Vision sensors capture images through rear cameras to calculate the distance between vehicles. The system shares visual data related to safety distance in real time, even in low-penetration scenarios of connected vehicles. The approach influences drivers in maintaining a time gap of two seconds. They addressed the need for cooperative systems to enhance road safety, even when vehicle communication penetration rates are not 100%. It focuses on preventing rear-end collisions due to tailgating and discusses the importance of maintaining a safe distance. Their study extends a previous approach and proposes a rear-mounted distance warning system that collaboratively communicates with other vehicles without depending on their communication capabilities. The system's setup and objectives are outlined. The system setup, which runs on a Raspberry Pi or Linux-based microcomputer with cameras capturing images and performing vehicle detection using OpenCV, is described. Stereoscopic vision is used to calculate the distance between vehicles. The system is cost-efficient, using USB web cameras with a field of view of 60° and a mobile battery pack for power supply. Results from the evaluation of the in-vehicle VANET-based system and the Tailigator rear-mounted distance warning system are presented where both systems effectively prompted drivers to maintain a two-second time gap. However, drivers using the VANET system exhibited slightly lower speeds and more regular braking patterns, suggesting a potential distraction.                 Hsia, Cai, Pai, and Magid’s ​\[6\]​ study presented a driving safety system that dynamically adjusts the safe distance between vehicles based on speed and weather conditions. The system can determine and maintain safe distances in the front and rear of a car, improving road safety. They highlighted the importance of driving safety and identified insufficient safe distance as a significant cause of traffic accidents. They aimed to propose a driving safety system that considers factors like vehicle speed, weather conditions, and road slope to adjust safe distances dynamically. Their system architecture alerts drivers when there is insufficient safe distance from the vehicle in front. It modifies the safe distance rule based on environmental parameters like rain, fog, and slope. The system can be integrated into the driving computer or used as an independent driving safety assistant. The alarm unit includes audio and light alerts to remind drivers to slow down or act. A simulation experiment system was conducted to demonstrate the proposed technology which uses various sensors to detect front distance, throttle acceleration, and uphill/downhill states. An LCD display shows simulation results, and the alarm system is activated when the safe distance is insufficient. The proposed driving safety system has numerous advantages, including the automatic adjustment of safe distances based on speed and weather conditions and the ability to alert drivers to decelerate or change lanes in case of insufficient safe distances.                 Kamal, Imura, Hayakawa, Ohata, and Aihara ​\[7\]​ introduced a smart-driving system based on Model Predictive Control (MPC) to improve traffic flow and alleviate congestion in urban areas. The system predicts preceding traffic behavior to regulate safe inter-vehicle distances, reducing frequent acceleration and deceleration to attenuate jamming waves in dense traffic. It aims to address traffic congestion, wasted time, and increased emissions in urban settings. They discussed the use of Advanced Adaptive Cruise Control (ACC) and Cooperative ACC (CACC) as potential solutions for traffic flow improvement. It introduces the optimal velocity model (OVM) to simulate human-driven traffic behavior and its impact on jamming wave propagation. The smart-driving system within the MPC framework controls a single vehicle, predicting future trajectories of preceding and following vehicles to achieve gentle acceleration and deceleration and enhance traffic flow. Numerical simulations demonstrate the system's effectiveness, and potential future work includes cooperative driving with multiple smart vehicles and exploring nonuniform traffic scenarios.   |  |
| **HOW DO YOU PLAN TO SOLVE THE PROBLEM?          ** The proponents of this study would like to explore the possibility of using object detection with artificial intelligence and the YOLO (You Only Look Once) algorithm to assist drivers in vehicle tracking and distance calculation. The proposed application will be developed using datasets from MS COCO and a custom dataset to identify and track the different types of vehicles present on the road. The researchers aim to do this to aid drivers in maintaining the required safe following distance of cars when on the road. This will use Python as its programming language and OpenCV image processing library. The DeepSORT algorithm will support the YOLO algorithm to track the detected objects in the frame properly in real time. The application will display the labels of the tracked object and the distance between the camera and the vehicles around it, especially the car in front, and will alert drivers if the required safe following distance is not observed. |  |
